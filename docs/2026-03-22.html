Math Research Digest

Welcome to this week's edition of Grounded, a particularly rich issue spanning arithmetic geometry, combinatorics, and analysis. Two threads stand out immediately: first, a satisfying resolution to a problem Manin posed over fifty years ago, with new results on R-equivalence confirming that rational points on cubic surfaces over 2-adic fields are far more connected than previously provable — a reminder that classical questions in algebraic geometry can still yield to fresh techniques. Second, the combinatorics papers this week share a striking preoccupation with *positivity phenomena*, from gamma vectors of flag simplicial spheres to the magic positivity of Ehrhart polynomials for parking-function polytopes; in both cases, the surprise is how strong structural consequences — real-rootedness, log-concavity, sharp growth bounds — follow from what look like elementary non-negativity conditions. Rounding things out, the number theory contribution on multiplication tables for integers with restricted prime factors identifies a sharp phase transition at a critical density parameter of 1/2, the kind of clean threshold behavior that feels inevitable in retrospect but requires serious analytic machinery to pin down precisely.

$R$-equivalence on Cubic Surfaces I: Existing Cases with Non-Trivial Universal Equivalence

Dimitri Kanevsky, Julian Salazar, Matt Harvey

## R-Equivalence on Cubic Surfaces Two points on an algebraic variety are **R-equivalent** if you can connect them by a chain of rational curves (copies of the projective line). The set of R-equivalence classes measures how "connected" a variety's rational points are — triviality means all rational points are linked this way. This paper proves R-equivalence is trivial (or nearly so) on cubic surfaces over 2-adic fields that were previously resistant to analysis, resolving a question Manin posed in 1972 and supporting a major conjecture about rational points on algebraic surfaces.

Rigorous Error Certification for Neural PDE Solvers: From Empirical Residuals to Solution Guarantees

Amartya Mukherjee, Maxwell Fitzsimmons, David C. Del Rey Fernández et al.

Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) solve differential equations by training a neural network to satisfy the equation's rules rather than using traditional grid-based methods — but until now, nobody could rigorously guarantee how close their outputs are to the true solution. This paper proves that if a PINN's residual error (how badly it fails to satisfy the equation pointwise) vanishes, and the network stays within a compact region of solution space, then the approximation provably converges to the true solution. This bridges a critical gap between empirical performance and certified reliability for neural PDE solvers.

cs.LG math.AP math.FA
Original abstract

Uncertainty quantification for partial differential equations is traditionally grounded in discretization theory, where solution error is controlled via mesh/grid refinement. Physics-informed neural networks fundamentally depart from this paradigm: they approximate solutions by minimizing residual losses at collocation points, introducing new sources of error arising from optimization, sampling, representation, and overfitting. As a result, the generalization error in the solution space remains an open problem. Our main theoretical contribution establishes generalization bounds that connect residual control to solution-space error. We prove that when neural approximations lie in a compact subset of the solution space, vanishing residual error guarantees convergence to the true solution. We derive deterministic and probabilistic convergence results and provide certified generalization bounds translating residual, boundary, and initial errors into explicit solution error guarantees.

Remarks on Brunn-Minkowski-type inequalities related to the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck operator

Francisco Marín Sola, Francesco Salerno

The Brunn-Minkowski theorem classically says that geometric quantities like volume behave in a "concave" way when you blend two convex shapes together via Minkowski addition (forming all pairwise sums of their points). This paper asks whether similar inequalities hold for two quantities tied to the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck operator—a differential operator central to probability and physics—namely torsional rigidity and first eigenvalue. The answer is largely *no*: the paper constructs explicit counterexamples showing concavity, convexity, and log-variants all fail, resolving several open questions negatively, with one positive exception for balls.

A Variational Approach to Degenerate Monge--Ampère Equations with Mixed Measures and Monotonicity

Nam Q. Le

The Monge–Ampère equation generalizes the determinant of second derivatives of a convex function, and finding its "eigenvalue" means finding a scalar λ where the equation balances a given measure. This paper proves when such problems are solvable and unique—even for badly singular measures—and shows that when classical energy-class solutions fail to exist, the eigenvalue can still be approximated via truncated measures and iterative schemes. The results expose surprising phenomena: highly singular measures can force symmetry-breaking and non-unique solutions.

math.AP
Original abstract

We study the solvability and uniqueness for several degenerate Monge--Ampère equations including the Monge--Ampère eigenvalue problem in real Euclidean spaces that involve singular Borel measures. Our approach systematically analyzes the Monge--Ampère energy from the variational point of view and appropriately exploits monotonicity arguments. Our main tools consist of the mixed Monge--Ampère measure, Aleksandrov--Blocki--Jerison-type maximum principles, integration by parts, convex envelope, and comparison principles for subcritical equations. For the Monge--Ampère eigenvalue problem, we contrast the analysis within and without the energy class; even if it might not have solutions in the energy class, we show that the infimum of the Rayleigh quotient can be approximated from above by Monge--Ampère eigenvalues of the truncated measures, and by Rayleigh quotients of an inverse iterative scheme. We give examples showing that for very singular Borel measures, the Monge--Ampère eigenvalue problem has only solutions outside the energy class together with symmetry breaking and nonuniqueness.

Well-posedness for the Navier-Stokes equations in Morrey spaces on non-compact manifolds

Víctor Chaves-Santos, Lucas C. F. Ferreira

Can fluid flow equations be solved on curved, infinite spaces? This paper proves that the Navier-Stokes equations — which govern incompressible fluid flow — are well-posed on negatively curved, non-compact manifolds like hyperbolic space. Negative curvature actually *improves* the equations' behavior by accelerating decay of solutions over time. The key advance is using **Morrey spaces** (function spaces that measure local concentration rather than global size), which admit rougher, non-decaying initial data that classical Lebesgue-space approaches cannot handle.

math.AP
Original abstract

We analyze the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations on a class of non-compact Riemannian manifolds within the framework of Morrey spaces. Assuming bounded geometry together with negative Ricci and sectional curvature (e.g., hyperbolic spaces), we establish dispersive and smoothing estimates for the heat semigroups associated with the Beltrami, Bochner and Hodge Laplacians in Morrey spaces, as well as for the Riesz transform. In particular, the presence of negative curvature yields improved large-time decay compared to the Euclidean setting. These estimates are of independent interest and enable us to construct solutions in time-weighted spaces of Kato type, leading to local-in-time well-posedness on a broad class of non-compact manifolds and global one in the case of Einstein manifolds. In the latter setting, we assume a smallness condition on the initial data in Morrey norms, which are weaker than -norms and thus allow for certain classes of large -data. We also discuss extensions to Ricci-flat manifolds. Our results introduce a new class of non-decaying and rough initial data for the Navier-Stokes equations on manifolds, extending previous works in Lebesgue and Sobolev spaces.

Gamma positivity, PL homeomorphism types, and orthogonal polynomials

**Gamma positivity** asks whether certain numerical invariants of geometric objects (simplicial spheres — triangulated surfaces generalizing ordinary spheres) can be written using only non-negative coefficients in a specific polynomial basis. This paper investigates how **flagness** (a combinatorial condition requiring every minimal "missing face" to have exactly two vertices) quantitatively affects these invariants. The key finding is a dichotomy: in high dimensions, flagness often has negligible effect on gamma vectors, but when it does matter, it forces measurable lower bounds on how fast these invariants grow.

Hamiltonian Reduction in Affine Principal Bundles

Miguel Ángel Berbel, Marco Castrillón López

**Reducing Symmetry in Field Theories Without Choosing a Reference Frame** When a physical field theory has symmetry, you can "quotient out" that symmetry to get a simpler description — this is called Hamiltonian reduction. This paper develops that procedure for theories living on *affine principal bundles* (spaces where the symmetry group acts by affine, not just linear, transformations), crucially without requiring a *connection* (a choice of "reference direction"). The result gives cleaner reduced equations of motion, validated on the physical example of molecular strands.

Original abstract

This paper presents a Hamiltonian reduction procedure for field theories over affine principal bundles introducing a canonical identification to describe the reduced multisymplectic space without the introduction of a connection. The main goal is to provide a Hamiltonian analogue of the Lagrangian reduction theory developed in M. Castrillón López, P. M. Chacón, and P. L. García. J. Geom. Mech., 5(4):399-414, 2013. The core of this work lies in the derivation of this canonical identification, the reduced Hamilton-Cartan equations, and a reduced covariant bracket that describes the dynamics. Finally, this theoretical framework is illustrated with a fundamental example: molecular strands.

**Quantum Block Encoding for Semiseparable Matrices** Quantum algorithms often need to "load" a matrix into a quantum computer by embedding it inside a larger unitary operation — a process called *block encoding*. This matters because semiseparable matrices appear throughout scientific computing, and fast quantum encoding could unlock speedups for solving structured linear systems.

A stable and fast method for solving multibody scattering problems via the method of fundamental solutions

Yunhui Cai, Joar Bagge, Per-Gunnar Martinsson

When sound waves hit multiple objects, computing how they scatter is expensive — the global system of equations tends to become numerically unstable as the number of objects grows. This paper shows you can compute a "scattering matrix" (how each individual object redirects incoming waves) locally using the Method of Fundamental Solutions — a simple but normally unstable technique — then combine these matrices into a well-conditioned global system that scales efficiently. The result: accurate, stable, large-scale scattering simulations without the implementation headaches of more rigorous boundary integral methods.

math.NA math-ph physics.comp-ph
Original abstract

The paper describes a numerical method for solving acoustic multibody scattering problems in two and three dimensions. The idea is to compute a highly accurate approximation to the scattering operator for each body through a local computation, and then use these scattering matrices to form a global linear system. The resulting coefficient matrix is relatively well-conditioned, even for problems involving a very large number of scatterers. The linear system is amenable to iterative solvers, and can readily be accelerated via fast algorithms for the matrix-vector multiplication such as the fast multipole method. The key point of the work is that the local scattering matrices can be constructed using potentially ill-conditioned techniques such as the method of fundamental solutions (MFS), while still maintaining scalability and numerical stability of the global solver. The resulting algorithm is simple, as the MFS is far simpler to implement than alternative techniques based on discretizing boundary integral equations using Nyström or Galerkin.

Numerical Considerations for the Construction of Karhunen-Loève Expansions

Cosmin Safta, Habib N. Najm

**How do you efficiently decompose a random field into its essential "modes" of variation?** The Karhunen-Loève expansion (KLE) breaks a random process into orthogonal components ranked by importance — think of it as PCA for functions — by solving an eigenvalue problem on the process's covariance structure. This paper shows that two seemingly different computational approaches (discretizing the integral equation vs. doing SVD on data matrices) are mathematically equivalent, then benchmarks both across tricky geometries including donut-shaped domains where "distance" must be measured through the interior, not through empty space.

math.NA stat.ML
Original abstract

This report examines numerical aspects of constructing Karhunen-Loève expansions (KLEs) for second-order stochastic processes. The KLE relies on the spectral decomposition of the covariance operator via the Fredholm integral equation of the second kind, which is then discretized on a computational grid, leading to an eigendecomposition task. We derive the algebraic equivalence between this Fredholm-based eigensolution and the singular value decomposition of the weight-scaled sample matrix, yielding consistent solutions for both model-based and data-driven KLE construction. Analytical eigensolutions for exponential and squared-exponential covariance kernels serve as reference benchmarks to assess numerical consistency and accuracy in 1D settings. The convergence of SVD-based eigenvalue estimates and of the empirical distributions of the KL coefficients to their theoretical target are characterized as a function of sample count. Higher-dimensional configurations include a two-dimensional irregular domain discretized by unstructured triangular meshes with two refinement levels, and a three-dimensional toroidal domain whose non-simply-connected topology motivates a comparison between Euclidean and shortest interior path distances between the grid points. The numerical results highlight the interplay between the discretization strategy, quadrature rule, and sample count, and their impact on the KLE results.

Duality of generalized Maxwell theories as an equivalence in derived geometry

Chris Elliott, Owen Gwilliam, Ingmar Saberi et al.

Generalized Maxwell theories extend classical electromagnetism to higher-dimensional analogues of electric and magnetic fields. The variety of Lie algebra representations

Bruna Mariana Braido da Silva Percinotti

**What's the question?** Given a Lie algebra (a vector space encoding infinitesimal symmetries with a bracket operation), how do all its representations—structure-preserving maps into a fixed "nice" (semisimple) Lie algebra—fit together geometrically? **Key result:** Every representation of a semisimple Lie algebra minimizes a natural energy function on its orbit, yielding clean new proofs of two classical theorems: rigidity of semisimple homomorphisms and Mostow's theorem on compatible Cartan involutions. **Why it matters:** This reframes classical structural theorems through variational calculus, potentially unlocking new tools for understanding representation spaces more broadly.

math.RT
Original abstract

We study the affine variety of Lie algebra representations, the collection of all homomorphisms from an arbitrary -dimensional Lie algebra into a fixed real semi-simple Lie algebra . Using techniques from real Geometric Invariant Theory, we equip this variety with a natural moment map and associated energy functional arising from the action of the real reductive group . We analyze the critical points of the energy functional and describe their structure. In particular, we prove that every semi-simple pair, that is representations of semi-simple Lie algebras, will globally minimize the energy in its orbit. As consequences, we obtain an elementary proof of the rigidity of semi-simple homomorphisms and derive a new proof of the Mostow theorem on the existence of compatible Cartan involutions for semi-simple subalgebras. ADMM-Based Distributed MPC with Control Barrier Functions for Safe Multi-Robot Quadrupedal Locomotion

Yicheng Zeng, Ruturaj S. Sambhus, Basit Muhammad Imran et al.

**Safe robot teamwork without a central brain** When multiple legged robots must navigate together without colliding, the math ensuring safety couples all robots' decisions together—making the optimization problem hard to split up. This paper reformulates the problem using ADMM (a technique for breaking coupled optimization into independently-solvable pieces) so each robot only needs to communicate with its neighbors. The result matches centralized planning quality while cutting computation time by 51% for four robots, enabling real-time safe coordination.

cs.RO math.OC
Original abstract

This paper proposes a fully decentralized model predictive control (MPC) framework with control barrier function (CBF) constraints for safety-critical trajectory planning in multi-robot legged systems. The incorporation of CBF constraints introduces explicit inter-agent coupling, which prevents direct decomposition of the resulting optimal control problems. To address this challenge, we reformulate the centralized safety-critical MPC problem using a structured distributed optimization framework based on the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM). By introducing a novel node-edge splitting formulation with consensus constraints, the proposed approach decomposes the global problem into independent node-local and edge-local quadratic programs that can be solved in parallel using only neighbor-to-neighbor communication. This enables fully decentralized trajectory optimization with symmetric computational load across agents while preserving safety and dynamic feasibility. The proposed framework is integrated into a hierarchical locomotion control architecture for quadrupedal robots, combining high-level distributed trajectory planning, mid-level nonlinear MPC enforcing single rigid body dynamics, and low-level whole-body control enforcing full-order robot dynamics. The results show that the proposed distributed formulation achieves performance comparable to centralized MPC while reducing the average per-cycle planning time by up to 51% in the four-agent case, enabling efficient real-time decentralized implementation.

**What's the question?** Many data science tasks—image processing, machine learning—require decomposing a matrix into a product of factors that share a specific symmetric structure, a notoriously difficult nonconvex optimization problem. **What did they find?** The authors prove that adding a penalty term to enforce symmetry works exactly (not approximately), then build an efficient algorithm (A-NAUM) that provably converges to a solution. **Why it matters?** This unifies and accelerates a broad family of matrix factorization problems under one rigorous framework with convergence guarantees.

**How do you efficiently schedule a "virtual power plant" — a network of solar panels, batteries, and generators — when prices, demand, and renewable output are all uncertain?** Traditional stochastic model predictive control (MPC), which optimizes decisions over many future scenarios simultaneously, becomes computationally impossible as the system scales up. This paper combines MPC with *time series aggregation* (compressing the planning horizon into fewer time steps) and distributed optimization (splitting the problem across assets), cutting runtimes by over 50% while providing proven bounds on how much accuracy is lost. **Why it matters:** Real-world renewable energy dispatch is currently intractable at scale — this framework makes it solvable.

Original abstract

This paper addresses the energy dispatch of a virtual power plant comprising renewable generation, energy storage, and thermal units under uncertainty in renewable output, energy prices, and energy demand. The nonlinear dynamics and multiple sources of uncertainty render traditional stochastic model predictive control (MPC) computationally intractable as the dispatch horizon, scenario set, and asset portfolio expand. To overcome this limitation, we propose a novel controller that seamlessly integrates MPC with time series aggregation and distributed optimization, simultaneously reducing the temporal, asset, and scenario dimensions of the problem. The resulting controller provides a rigorous performance guarantee through theoretically validated bounds on its approximation error, while leveraging dual information from previous MPC iterations to adaptively optimize the temporal aggregation. Numerical results show that the proposed controller reduces runtime by over 50% relative to traditional stochastic MPC and, crucially, restores tractability where the full-scale dispatch model proves intractable.

Furstenberg-type estimates under mild non-concentration assumptions

Tuomas Orponen, Pablo Shmerkin

**When lines cluster around sets, how large must those sets be?** The Furstenberg set problem asks: if a set in the plane contains a long piece of a line pointing in each of many directions, how large must the set be? This paper proves sharp lower bounds on such sets' size (measured via Hausdorff dimension) using weaker assumptions than before — specifically, relaxing *non-concentration* conditions, which prevent points or directions from clumping together. This expands the settings where precise dimension estimates are guaranteed.

Spectral continuity of almost commutative manifolds for the $C^1$ topology on Riemannian metrics

Frederic Latremoliere

Almost commutative manifolds are geometric objects built by combining a classical curved space (a spin manifold, where you can define a notion of "square root of the Laplacian" called a Dirac operator) with a small finite-dimensional quantum component — a structure used in Connes' geometric reformulation of particle physics. This paper proves that the eigenvalues of the Dirac operator vary continuously as you smoothly deform the underlying geometry. Since the physics is encoded in that spectrum, this guarantees the models are geometrically stable under perturbation.

math.OA math-ph
Original abstract

Almost commutative models provide a framework for Connes' work on the standard model of particle physics. These models are constructed as products of a the canonical spectral triple of a compact connected spin manifold with a finite dimensional spectral triple. Motivated by the fundamental question of the dependence of the spectra of Dirac operators under change of metrics, we prove the continuity of the spectra of Dirac operators for almost commutative models as functions of the underlying Riemannian metric. We allow both the Riemannian metric (in the topology) and the Dirac operator of the finite-dimensional factor to vary simultaneously. Since the physics of the system is fundamentally encoded in this spectrum, this result is a form of stability result regarding the geometry, or physical, content of these models. This result is based upon a novel approach to prove continuity of spectra of Dirac operators using the spectral propinquity. Notably, this method provides a new, different proof of the classical results as well. To illustrate the versatility of our new method, we also apply our results to completely non-commutative family of examples, including quantum tori and quantum solenoids.

On the asymptotic behavior of finite hyperfields

Tuong Le, Chayim Lowen

A **hyperfield** generalizes a field by allowing addition to return a *set* of possible values rather than a single element. Some hyperfields arise naturally as quotients of ordinary fields, but are most of them built this way? This paper proves they almost never are — as hyperfields grow large, nearly all of them are "non-quotient," confirming a conjecture of Baker–Jin. The key tool is a probabilistic argument counting hyperfields on finite abelian groups, with precise asymptotics provided.

math.RA
Original abstract

Hobby has recently shown that almost all finite hyperfields of even order fail to be the quotient of a field. Using a probabilistic argument, we extend this result to all orders: a finite hyperfield is almost always non-quotient. This confirms a conjecture of Baker--Jin. We show that in almost every finite hyperfield the sum of any four or more nonzero elements contains 0. We also give a precise asymptotic for the number of finite hyperfields on a given finite abelian group.
