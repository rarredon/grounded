Welcome to this week's edition of Grounded. The papers arriving in our inbox this week span an unusually wide arc — from half-century-old open problems to the foundations of machine learning — but two themes stand out as especially striking. First, the arithmetic of divisors and rational points continues to yield beautiful surprises: a 50-year-old question about R-equivalence on cubic surfaces over 2-adic fields has finally been resolved, while a sharp phase transition in the distribution of smooth-restricted integers has been pinned down with the kind of explicit critical-point precision that rarely appears in multiplicative number theory. Multiplication Tables for Integers with Restricted Prime Factors

Jeremy Schlitt

**How many numbers have a "smooth" divisor?** If you restrict your integers to only have prime factors from a chosen set of primes, how often does such a number have a divisor landing in a specific interval? This paper determines the exact order of magnitude of this count for all interval sizes, generalizing Ford's 2008 work on unrestricted integers. Crucially, it identifies a sharp *phase transition* — a critical threshold where the counting function's behavior changes qualitatively — and pins down the precise behavior there. **Why it matters:** Phase transitions in multiplicative number theory reveal deep structural shifts in how divisors distribute, and explicit critical-point analysis is rare.

$R$-equivalence on Cubic Surfaces I: Existing Cases with Non-Trivial Universal Equivalence

Dimitri Kanevsky, Julian Salazar, Matt Harvey

**R-equivalence** asks whether any two rational points on a variety can be linked by a chain of rational curves (copies of the line). "Trivial R-equivalence" means all rational points are connected this way — a strong form of path-connectedness over the base field. This paper settles a 50-year-old question by proving R-equivalence is trivial on specific cubic surfaces over 2-adic fields that had resisted all prior methods, including Manin's explicitly posed case from 1972 — while revealing surprising connections to conjectures about rational parameterizations of these surfaces.

We consider all surfaces currently known to have non-trivial universal equivalence. Beyond being intractable to Swinnerton-Dyer's approach, we observe that if these surfaces also had non-trivial -equivalence, they would contradict Colliot-Thélène and Sansuc's conjecture regarding the -rationality of universal torsors for geometrically rational surfaces. By devising new methods to study -equivalence, we prove that for 2-adic surfaces with all-Eckardt reductions (the third special type, which contains every existing case of non-trivial universal equivalence), -equivalence is trivial or of exponent 2. Remarks on Brunn-Minkowski-type inequalities related to the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck operator

Francisco Marín Sola, Francesco Salerno

The Brunn-Minkowski inequality is a classical result saying that geometric quantities like volume behave in a "concave" way when you combine two shapes via Minkowski addition (sliding one shape's boundary along another's). This paper asks whether similar inequalities hold for two quantities tied to the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck operator—a differential operator central to probability theory—called torsional rigidity and the first eigenvalue. The answer is mostly no: the paper constructs explicit counterexamples showing these quantities are neither concave, convex, nor log-concave in general, resolving several open questions in the field.

A Variational Approach to Degenerate Monge--Ampère Equations with Mixed Measures and Monotonicity

Nam Q. Le

The Monge–Ampère equation is a nonlinear PDE involving the determinant of a function's second-derivative matrix; it appears in optimal transport and geometry. This paper asks: when can you solve these equations with highly singular measures (think mass concentrated on low-dimensional sets) as data, and are solutions unique? The authors develop a variational framework—optimizing an energy functional over convex functions—and show that even when classical solutions fail, the fundamental eigenvalue can be approximated systematically, while very singular measures can force symmetry-breaking and non-uniqueness.

math.AP
Original abstract

We study the solvability and uniqueness for several degenerate Monge--Ampère equations including the Monge--Ampère eigenvalue problem in real Euclidean spaces that involve singular Borel measures. Our approach systematically analyzes the Monge--Ampère energy from the variational point of view and appropriately exploits monotonicity arguments. Our main tools consist of the mixed Monge--Ampère measure, Aleksandrov--Blocki--Jerison-type maximum principles, integration by parts, convex envelope, and comparison principles for subcritical equations. For the Monge--Ampère eigenvalue problem, we contrast the analysis within and without the energy class; even if it might not have solutions in the energy class, we show that the infimum of the Rayleigh quotient can be approximated from above by Monge--Ampère eigenvalues of the truncated measures, and by Rayleigh quotients of an inverse iterative scheme. We give examples showing that for very singular Borel measures, the Monge--Ampère eigenvalue problem has only solutions outside the energy class together with symmetry breaking and nonuniqueness.

Well-posedness for the Navier-Stokes equations in Morrey spaces on non-compact manifolds

Víctor Chaves-Santos, Lucas C. F. Ferreira

**Can fluids be well-modeled on curved, infinite spaces?** This paper proves that the Navier-Stokes equations — which govern viscous fluid flow — have unique, stable solutions on a broad class of infinite curved spaces (non-compact Riemannian manifolds), including hyperbolic space. The key tool is *Morrey spaces*, function spaces that measure local concentration of a quantity while allowing non-decaying behavior globally, making them strictly weaker than standard Lᵖ spaces and enabling rougher, larger initial data. Negative curvature — intuitively meaning the space "spreads out" faster than flat Euclidean space — actually helps by accelerating how quickly disturbances decay, yielding better long-time behavior than in the flat setting.

math.AP
Original abstract

We analyze the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations on a class of non-compact Riemannian manifolds within the framework of Morrey spaces. Assuming bounded geometry together with negative Ricci and sectional curvature (e.g., hyperbolic spaces), we establish dispersive and smoothing estimates for the heat semigroups associated with the Beltrami, Bochner and Hodge Laplacians in Morrey spaces, as well as for the Riesz transform. In particular, the presence of negative curvature yields improved large-time decay compared to the Euclidean setting. These estimates are of independent interest and enable us to construct solutions in time-weighted spaces of Kato type, leading to local-in-time well-posedness on a broad class of non-compact manifolds and global one in the case of Einstein manifolds. In the latter setting, we assume a smallness condition on the initial data in Morrey norms, which are weaker than -norms and thus allow for certain classes of large -data. We also discuss extensions to Ricci-flat manifolds. Our results introduce a new class of non-decaying and rough initial data for the Navier-Stokes equations on manifolds, extending previous works in Lebesgue and Sobolev spaces.

Gamma positivity, PL homeomorphism types, and orthogonal polynomials

Soohyun Park

Simplicial spheres are high-dimensional triangulated shapes homeomorphic to ordinary spheres. Their combinatorial complexity is captured by *h-vectors* (encoding face counts) and *gamma vectors* (a refined decomposition). A central open question is whether "flag" spheres—those with no "missing faces" of size two—always have nonnegative gamma vectors. This paper shows that flagness, measured via a topological criterion called the link condition, has surprisingly little quantitative effect on gamma vectors in high dimensions, but provides explicit growth-rate lower bounds when it does matter, with connections to orthogonal polynomials and combinatorial dimer-counting.

Original abstract

In particular, we show that the link condition has a trivial effect on the -vectors (and thus gamma vectors) of high-dimensional simplicial spheres with nonnegative gamma vectors in many cases. Note that this reflects a dichotomy between quantitative behavior arising from components that are linear in and those that are superlinear in . When the link condition is nontrivial, we show that it gives a lower bound for growth rates of -vector components. This lower bound increases with the number of edges and the distance of the -vector condition on -vectors of simplicial spheres from equality. These lower bounds translate to ones on top gamma vector components and give lower bounds on gamma vector growth rates when the gamma vector components are dominant terms in the -vector components with the same index (e.g. -vectors with components increasing quickly compared to ). Finally, we show that the same results apply to positivity properties generalizing gamma positivity arising from connections between orthogonal polynomials and lattice paths. In the course of doing this, we describe gamma vector components in terms of monomer/dimer covers and point out connections between repeated (stellar) edge subdivisions (Tchebyshev subdivisions) and dimer covers.

Luck and magic for Pitman-Stanley polytopes and parking functions

Nicolas Avila, Luis Ferroni, Alejandro H. Morales

## Summary How do you count lattice points (points with whole-number coordinates) inside certain geometric shapes called Pitman-Stanley polytopes, and what structure do those counts reveal? The authors prove these polytopes satisfy "magic positivity" — meaning their Ehrhart polynomials (which count lattice points as the polytope is scaled up) have positive coefficients in a special basis — and connect this to counting "lucky cars" in a generalized parking process. **Why it matters:** Magic positivity implies the h*-polynomials (generating functions encoding lattice-point counts) are real-rooted, guaranteeing their coefficients are log-concave and unimodal — highly desirable properties that are often difficult to establish and signal deep combinatorial structure.

math.CO
Original abstract

Motivated by the combinatorics of parking functions and their several generalizations, we study the Ehrhart theory of Pitman--Stanley polytopes. We prove a strong positivity phenomenon called magic positivity for the Ehrhart polynomials of these polytopes, which in turn implies that their -polynomials are real-rooted (and thus log-concave and unimodal). Our result is achieved by interpreting the coefficients of these Ehrhart polynomials in the magic basis in terms of the number of lucky cars in a modified parking protocol. Furthermore, we address the magic positivity problem for -generalized permutohedra and also discuss a magic combinatorial interpretation for them, under the assumption that the input parameters are sufficiently large.

Hamiltonian Reduction in Affine Principal Bundles

Miguel Ángel Berbel, Marco Castrillón López

**Reducing Symmetry in Field Theories Without Extra Baggage** When a physical field theory has symmetry, you can "mod out" that symmetry to get a simpler description — this is called **Hamiltonian reduction**. But standard methods require choosing an arbitrary geometric structure called a **connection**. This paper develops a reduction procedure for a special class of symmetries (**affine principal bundles**) that works canonically, without that arbitrary choice. The result gives cleaner reduced equations of motion, demonstrated concretely on models of elastic molecular strands.

math.DG math-ph math.DS
Original abstract

This paper presents a Hamiltonian reduction procedure for field theories over affine principal bundles introducing a canonical identification to describe the reduced multisymplectic space without the introduction of a connection. The main goal is to provide a Hamiltonian analogue of the Lagrangian reduction theory developed in M. Castrillón López, P. M. Chacón, and P. L. García. J. Geom. Mech., 5(4):399-414, 2013. The core of this work lies in the derivation of this canonical identification, the reduced Hamilton-Cartan equations, and a reduced covariant bracket that describes the dynamics. Finally, this theoretical framework is illustrated with a fundamental example: molecular strands.

Numerical Analysis

A stable and fast method for solving multibody scattering problems via the method of fundamental solutions

Yunhui Cai, Joar Bagge, Per-Gunnar Martinsson

## Bouncing Waves Off Many Objects at Once When a sound wave hits multiple objects, it scatters off each one and then hits the others repeatedly — making "multibody scattering" computationally brutal. This paper introduces a method that computes how each individual object scatters waves (its **scattering matrix**) separately, then assembles these into a stable global system. Crucially, it allows using the simple but notoriously numerically unstable **Method of Fundamental Solutions** locally without that instability infecting the overall calculation — giving you simplicity without sacrificing reliability at scale.

math.NA math-ph physics.comp-ph
Original abstract

The paper describes a numerical method for solving acoustic multibody scattering problems in two and three dimensions. The idea is to compute a highly accurate approximation to the scattering operator for each body through a local computation, and then use these scattering matrices to form a global linear system. The resulting coefficient matrix is relatively well-conditioned, even for problems involving a very large number of scatterers. The linear system is amenable to iterative solvers, and can readily be accelerated via fast algorithms for the matrix-vector multiplication such as the fast multipole method. The key point of the work is that the local scattering matrices can be constructed using potentially ill-conditioned techniques such as the method of fundamental solutions (MFS), while still maintaining scalability and numerical stability of the global solver. The resulting algorithm is simple, as the MFS is far simpler to implement than alternative techniques based on discretizing boundary integral equations using Nyström or Galerkin.

Numerical Considerations for the Construction of Karhunen-Loève Expansions

Cosmin Safta, Habib N. Najm

**How do you efficiently decompose random fields into their essential components?** The Karhunen-Loève expansion (KLE) breaks a random process into ordered, uncorrelated "modes" — think of it as a principal component analysis for functions. This paper shows that two seemingly different computational approaches (solving a Fredholm integral equation versus taking a singular value decomposition of data matrices) are algebraically equivalent, unifying model-based and data-driven methods. The results clarify how mesh quality, numerical integration rules, and sample size jointly determine accuracy, with tests on complex 2D and 3D geometries.

math.NA stat.ML
Original abstract

This report examines numerical aspects of constructing Karhunen-Loève expansions (KLEs) for second-order stochastic processes. The KLE relies on the spectral decomposition of the covariance operator via the Fredholm integral equation of the second kind, which is then discretized on a computational grid, leading to an eigendecomposition task. We derive the algebraic equivalence between this Fredholm-based eigensolution and the singular value decomposition of the weight-scaled sample matrix, yielding consistent solutions for both model-based and data-driven KLE construction. Analytical eigensolutions for exponential and squared-exponential covariance kernels serve as reference benchmarks to assess numerical consistency and accuracy in 1D settings. The convergence of SVD-based eigenvalue estimates and of the empirical distributions of the KL coefficients to their theoretical target are characterized as a function of sample count. Higher-dimensional configurations include a two-dimensional irregular domain discretized by unstructured triangular meshes with two refinement levels, and a three-dimensional toroidal domain whose non-simply-connected topology motivates a comparison between Euclidean and shortest interior path distances between the grid points. The numerical results highlight the interplay between the discretization strategy, quadrature rule, and sample count, and their impact on the KLE results.

Reduced order computation of 2D elastodynamic Green's functions in layered soil using a low-rank tensor approximation

Zainab Farooq, Amar Pashov, Pieter Reumers et al.

**Simulating ground vibrations efficiently across many scenarios** When engineers model how seismic or mechanical waves travel through layered soil, they need "Green's functions" — mathematical recipes describing how vibrations propagate from a source to a receiver. Computing these across thousands of locations, frequencies, and material configurations is brutally expensive. This paper uses a tensor decomposition technique (compressing a high-dimensional data array into a compact product of simpler pieces) to represent all these scenarios simultaneously, cutting memory and computation dramatically without sacrificing accuracy.

math.NA
Original abstract

The evaluation of elastodynamic Green's functions across numerous source-receiver locations, frequencies, and material properties, particularly in the context of parametric studies or boundary element computations, is computationally demanding and memory intensive. This paper presents a reduced order modeling strategy based on the Greedy Tucker Approximation (GTA), which incrementally constructs a low-rank representation of the Green's tensor through rank-one enrichments obtained via a Proper Generalized Decomposition (PGD)-type alternating least squares procedure. A Petrov-Galerkin formulation is employed to improve convergence and approximation accuracy. The resulting multi-dimensional tensor, expressed in terms of one-dimensional basis functions and a compact core, achieves substantial reductions in memory requirements. The methodology is demonstrated for two cases: a soil layer on rigid bedrock and a layered halfspace. Different separable dimensions are considered to capture various combinations of source and receiver configurations, frequencies, and material parameters. Results are validated against those obtained with the direct stiffness method and computation times and memory requirements are compared.

The variety of Lie algebra representations

Bruna Mariana Braido da Silva Percinotti

**Core question:** Given a Lie algebra (a vector space encoding infinitesimal symmetries with a bracket operation), how do its representations—ways of mapping it into a fixed "nice" (semi-simple) Lie algebra—organize geometrically? **Key result:** Semi-simple representations (those with no "redundant flat directions") always minimize an energy functional on their orbit, yielding clean new proofs of two classical theorems: rigidity of semi-simple maps and Mostow's theorem on compatible Cartan involutions. **Why it matters:** This reframes classical structural theorems through modern geometric optimization, potentially unlocking new tools for studying representation spaces broadly.

math.RT
Original abstract

We study the affine variety of Lie algebra representations, the collection of all homomorphisms from an arbitrary -dimensional Lie algebra into a fixed real semi-simple Lie algebra . Using techniques from real Geometric Invariant Theory, we equip this variety with a natural moment map and associated energy functional arising from the action of the real reductive group . We analyze the critical points of the energy functional and describe their structure. In particular, we prove that every semi-simple pair, that is representations of semi-simple Lie algebras, will globally minimize the energy in its orbit. As consequences, we obtain an elementary proof of the rigidity of semi-simple homomorphisms and derive a new proof of the Mostow theorem on the existence of compatible Cartan involutions for semi-simple subalgebras. Subsequent results concerning the structure of critical points of higher energy are also obtained.

Embeddings of variable Sobolev, Besov, and Triebel-Lizorkin spaces on metric measure spaces

Ryan Alvarado, Michał Dymek, Przemysław Górka et al.

**Variable Smoothness Meets Variable Geometry** Classical Sobolev embedding theorems tell you when a function's smoothness guarantees it belongs to a "nicer" function space — but they assume the domain has uniform geometric structure. This paper asks: what happens when both the smoothness requirements *and* the geometry of the space vary from point to point? The authors build a complete embedding theory for variable-exponent function spaces on metric measure spaces, and remarkably show that the embeddings themselves *force* the underlying space to satisfy certain geometric growth conditions — making the analytic and geometric constraints genuinely equivalent.

Sobolev-type embeddings on metric measure spaces encode a subtle interaction between the analytic regularity of functions and the geometry of the underlying domain space. In this paper we develop an embedding theory for variable Hajłasz-type smoothness spaces on metric measure spaces whose ``dimension'' is allowed to vary pointwise through a bounded exponent that governs a lower Ahlfors growth condition on the measure. Fast and Effective Computation of Generalized Symmetric Matrix Factorization

Lei Yang, Han Wan, Min Zhang et al.

**Symmetric matrix factorization** — writing a matrix as a product of a matrix and its transpose — appears throughout machine learning and signal processing, but the underlying optimization problems are typically messy: non-convex, non-smooth, and lacking Lipschitz continuity (a standard smoothness condition algorithms rely on). This paper proves two structural "exactness" results that tame this messiness, then builds an efficient algorithm (A-NAUM) with proven convergence guarantees. The results unify a broad family of factorization problems under one framework, replacing ad hoc methods with principled, fast computation.

math.OC cs.LG
Original abstract

In this paper, we study a nonconvex, nonsmooth, and non-Lipschitz generalized symmetric matrix factorization model that unifies a broad class of matrix factorization formulations arising in machine learning, image science, engineering, and related areas. We first establish two exactness properties. On the modeling side, we prove an exact penalty property showing that, under suitable conditions, the symmetry-inducing quadratic penalty enforces symmetry whenever the penalty parameter is sufficiently large but finite, thereby exactly recovering the associated symmetric formulation. On the algorithmic side, we introduce an auxiliary-variable splitting formulation and establish an exact relaxation relationship that rigorously links stationary points of the original objective function to those of a relaxed potential function. Building on these exactness properties, we propose an average-type nonmonotone alternating updating method (A-NAUM) based on the relaxed potential function. At each iteration, A-NAUM alternately updates the two factor blocks by (approximately) minimizing the potential function, while the auxiliary block is updated in closed form. To ensure the convergence and enhance practical performance, we further incorporate an average-type nonmonotone line search and show that it is well-defined under mild conditions. Moreover, based on the Kurdyka-Łojasiewicz property and its associated exponent, we establish global convergence of the entire sequence to a stationary point and derive convergence rate results. Finally, numerical experiments on real datasets demonstrate the efficiency of A-NAUM.

Luca Santosuosso, Fei Teng, Sonja Wogrin

**How do you efficiently schedule a fleet of power sources when the future is uncertain?** A *virtual power plant* (VPP) pools renewable generators, batteries, and gas units into one coordinated system. Optimally scheduling them requires solving a massive stochastic optimization problem—one that becomes computationally impossible at realistic scales. This paper combines three complexity-reduction techniques (distributed optimization, time-aggregation, and scenario reduction) into a single controller, proving rigorous error bounds on the approximation and cutting runtimes by over 50% while restoring tractability where standard methods simply fail.

math.OC
Original abstract

This paper addresses the energy dispatch of a virtual power plant comprising renewable generation, energy storage, and thermal units under uncertainty in renewable output, energy prices, and energy demand. The nonlinear dynamics and multiple sources of uncertainty render traditional stochastic model predictive control (MPC) computationally intractable as the dispatch horizon, scenario set, and asset portfolio expand. Operator Algebras

1

Spectral continuity of almost commutative manifolds for the $C^1$ topology on Riemannian metrics

Frederic Latremoliere

Almost commutative manifolds blend ordinary curved geometry with finite algebraic structures to model particle physics. This paper proves that the eigenvalues of Dirac operators (which encode the system's physical content) vary continuously when you smoothly perturb the underlying Riemannian metric or finite-dimensional component. The key tool is *spectral propinquity*, a distance measure between operator-algebraic structures, which also yields a fresh proof of classical results and extends naturally to fully noncommutative spaces like quantum tori.

math.OA math-ph
Original abstract

Almost commutative models provide a framework for Connes' work on the standard model of particle physics. These models are constructed as products of a the canonical spectral triple of a compact connected spin manifold with a finite dimensional spectral triple. Motivated by the fundamental question of the dependence of the spectra of Dirac operators under change of metrics, we prove the continuity of the spectra of Dirac operators for almost commutative models as functions of the underlying Riemannian metric. We allow both the Riemannian metric (in the topology) and the Dirac operator of the finite-dimensional factor to vary simultaneously. Since the physics of the system is fundamentally encoded in this spectrum, this result is a form of stability result regarding the geometry, or physical, content of these models. This result is based upon a novel approach to prove continuity of spectra of Dirac operators using the spectral propinquity. Notably, this method provides a new, different proof of the classical results as well. To illustrate the versatility of our new method, we also apply our results to completely non-commutative family of examples, including quantum tori and quantum solenoids.

On the asymptotic behavior of finite hyperfields

Tuong Le, Chayim Lowen

**What's the question?** Hyperfields generalize ordinary fields by allowing addition to return a *set* of values rather than a single value. Some hyperfields arise naturally as quotients of ordinary fields, but are most hyperfields of this "quotient" type? **The result:** Almost none are — as hyperfields grow larger, the probability that a randomly chosen one is a quotient of a field drops to zero, confirming a conjecture of Baker–Jin. This reveals that quotient hyperfields are surprisingly rare exotic objects, not the norm.

math.RA
Original abstract

Hobby has recently shown that almost all finite hyperfields of even order fail to be the quotient of a field. Using a probabilistic argument, we extend this result to all orders: a finite hyperfield is almost always non-quotient. This confirms a conjecture of Baker--Jin. We show that in almost every finite hyperfield the sum of any four or more nonzero elements contains 0. We also give a precise asymptotic for the number of finite hyperfields on a given finite abelian group.
